Food Guide

Ghanaian cuisine is bold, flavorful, and deeply rooted in tradition. From fufu pounded by hand to jollof rice that sparks regional debates, here's your guide to the essential dishes that define Ghana's culinary soul.

Jollof Rice

Origin: West Africa · Ghana's version emphasizes Scotch bonnet peppers

Jollof rice is the dish that sparks friendly debate across West Africa. Ghana's version is known for its deep red-orange color, softer consistency, and bold use of Scotch bonnet peppers. The rice is cooked in a concentrated tomato-based stew with onions, spices like thyme, curry powder, ginger, and bay leaves,absorbing every ounce of flavor as it simmers. The best jollof develops a slightly caramelized crust at the bottom of the pot, called "socarrat." It's served with fried plantains, grilled chicken, or stews, and eaten at celebrations, Sunday gatherings, and any meal that matters.

Waakye

Origin: Northern Ghana · Now a national breakfast and lunch staple

Waakye is rice and beans cooked together with sorghum leaves, which give the dish its signature reddish-brown color. What makes waakye special is the symphony of accompaniments: fried plantains, spaghetti (called talia), boiled eggs, avocado, fried fish or beef, and shito, a fiery black pepper sauce made with dried fish and shrimp. Some vendors add gari foto (fried cassava flour) and a rich tomato-based meat stew. It's the ultimate one-plate meal, served wrapped in waakye leaves at street stalls across Ghana from dawn to early afternoon.

Fufu

Made from: Cassava & unripe plantains (or yam, cocoyam)

Fufu is Ghana's most iconic starchy staple, a smooth, stretchy, dough-like side dish made by pounding boiled cassava and unripe plantains with water in a large wooden mortar. The pounding process usually requires two people: one to pound, one to turn and fold the mixture. The result is a bland, gummy ball with a consistency that absorbs the rich flavors of whatever soup or stew it's paired with. Fufu is always served with soup,groundnut, light soup, palm nut soup, or kontomire. Tear off a piece, shape it with your fingers, dip it in the soup, and swallow without chewing. Never use your left hand.

Banku

Origin: Ga-Dangbe people · Made from fermented corn & cassava dough

Banku is a slightly sour, fermented dough made from corn and cassava flour, cooked in boiling water until it forms a smooth, elastic consistency. The fermentation gives banku its distinctive tangy flavor that pairs perfectly with rich, spicy accompaniments. It's a traditional meal of the Ga tribe, and the most iconic way to eat it is with grilled tilapia and hot ground pepper mixed with onions and tomatoes. You eat banku with your hands,pinch off a piece, dip it in the pepper sauce, and enjoy with flaky grilled fish. It's also served with okra stew, palm nut soup, or any pepper-heavy sauce.

Kenkey (Komi)

Variations: Ga kenkey (corn husk), Fante kenkey (plantain leaf)

Kenkey is fermented corn dough that's been wrapped and steamed, either in dried corn husks (Ga kenkey) or plantain leaves (Fante kenkey), which gives each version a different texture and subtle flavor. The dough is dense, slightly sour from fermentation, and has a firm, cake-like consistency. Kenkey is almost always eaten with fried fish,often herring or mackerel,and a fiery chili pepper sauce made with ground scotch bonnets, onions, and tomatoes. It's a coastal staple, sold at roadside stands and chop bars, and beloved for its filling, no-nonsense simplicity.

Kelewele

Made from: Ripe plantains · Spiced with ginger, chili, cloves

Kelewele is deep-fried ripe plantain cubes, seasoned with ginger, hot pepper, salt, and sometimes cloves, nutmeg, or cinnamon. The plantains are cut into chunks, tossed in the spice mix, and fried until golden and caramelized at the edges. The result is sweet, spicy, and slightly crispy on the outside with a soft, warm interior. Kelewele is eaten as a snack on its own, often with roasted peanuts, or as a side dish with bean stews, rice dishes, or waakye. It's sold by street vendors in the evening and shows up at parties as finger food. Simple, addictive, and unmistakably Ghanaian.
